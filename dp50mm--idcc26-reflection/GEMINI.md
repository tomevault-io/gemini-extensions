# Thematic Analysis of IDCC26 Poster Titles

## Method

### Approach

This analysis applies a lightweight inductive thematic analysis, loosely following Braun and Clarke's (2006) reflexive approach, to the 36 poster titles from the 20th International Digital Curation Conference (IDCC26), held 17–18 February 2026 in Zagreb, Croatia. The analysis was performed by an LLM (Claude, Anthropic) rather than a human researcher, at the request of a human analyst.

### Procedure

1. **Familiarization.** All 36 poster titles were read and re-read from the conference program (`program.md`).
2. **Open coding.** Key concepts, terms, and phrases were identified in each title (e.g., "FAIR," "training," "DMP," "sensitive data," "resilience," "AI").
3. **Theme generation.** Codes were grouped through iterative comparison into candidate themes based on conceptual coherence.
4. **Review and refinement.** Candidate themes were checked for internal coherence (do constituent posters share a recognizable concern?) and external distinctiveness (are themes meaningfully different from one another?). Themes were merged or split as needed.
5. **Cross-cutting observations.** Patterns that span multiple themes were noted separately.

### Scope and limitations

- **Title-only analysis.** This analysis is based exclusively on poster titles. Titles may emphasize novelty or appeal over substantive content, and many relevant aspects of the work (methods, findings, disciplinary framing) are invisible from titles alone. The themes identified here should be understood as reflecting what the poster authors chose to signal, not necessarily the full scope of the work.
- **LLM-performed.** This analysis was carried out by a large language model, not by a domain expert exercising reflexive judgment. The LLM brings broad pattern recognition but lacks the situated understanding, disciplinary expertise, and reflexive positionality that characterize human qualitative research. The results should be treated as a structured first pass, not as a finished qualitative study.
- **No inter-coder reliability.** There was a single "coder" (the LLM). No triangulation or member-checking was performed.
- **Poster 36** ("The Sarah Jones Award for exceptional contribution to fostering collaboration in Open Science") is an award rather than a research poster. It is included in the analysis because it was listed among the 36 posters and its title signals a thematic commitment.

## Themes

Seven themes were identified. Two posters (2 and 17) appear under two themes each, reflecting genuine dual focus visible in their titles. All 36 posters are accounted for.

## Cross-Cutting Observations

1. **People over technology.** Despite the conference's "digital" focus, the poster program tilts strongly toward human and organizational concerns — training, skills, collaboration, cultural change, mentorship. Technical infrastructure is often secondary to the social infrastructure needed to sustain it.

2. **FAIR as pervasive frame.** The FAIR acronym appears not only in Theme 2 but also in training titles (14, 33), DMP titles (6), preservation titles (9), and assessment titles (34). FAIR has become the lingua franca of the digital curation community, functioning as both a set of technical principles and a rhetorical marker of legitimacy.

3. **AI as both tool and provocation.** AI appears across themes — in DMP evaluation (poster 2), in training contexts (poster 17), in knowledge infrastructure (poster 1), and in domain-specific curation (poster 20). It is framed both as a practical tool and as a condition that demands new responses from the curation community. Notably, the conference's separate paper sessions on AI/ML suggest that the poster program's AI presence is modest relative to the broader conference.

4. **Political urgency and data rescue.** Posters 27 ("From Crisis to Coordination") and 30 ("Navigating Pressure") signal a community grappling with external political threats to research data and open science infrastructure, particularly in the US context. This urgency is a departure from the more routine organizational and technical concerns that characterize most of the poster program.

5. **Geographic breadth, European center of gravity.** The posters represent institutions from across Europe (Finland, Croatia, Portugal, Latvia, Slovenia, Belgium, Netherlands, Germany, Austria, Sweden, UK, Switzerland, France, Italy, Spain, Czech Republic), as well as Japan and the United States. The European dominance reflects both the conference's institutional home and the significant European investment in FAIR and EOSC infrastructure.

6. **Tension between standardization and local context.** Several posters navigate the tension between adopting global standards (FAIR, DDI) and accommodating local requirements — multilingual training resources (14, 33), national DMP templates (32), institutional silos (19), and national CRIS systems (21). Standardization is presented not as a given but as something that must be negotiated.

---

## Limitations

- This analysis is based on **titles only**. Titles are a lossy compression of research work. The themes identified here reflect what authors chose to signal in their titles, which may over-represent novelty and under-represent substantive content.
- The analysis was performed by a **large language model** (Claude, Anthropic), not a human qualitative researcher. While the procedure follows the general shape of Braun and Clarke's thematic analysis, it lacks the reflexive judgment, domain expertise, and iterative deliberation that characterize human-led qualitative research.
- **No abstracts, full texts, or author input** were consulted. A richer analysis would draw on these sources.
- The **theme boundaries are interpretive**. Other analysts — human or otherwise — might reasonably group these 36 titles differently. The two cross-listed posters (2, 17) illustrate cases where theme assignment is genuinely ambiguous.
- **Poster 36** is an award, not a research poster, and its inclusion slightly inflates the "collaboration" theme.
